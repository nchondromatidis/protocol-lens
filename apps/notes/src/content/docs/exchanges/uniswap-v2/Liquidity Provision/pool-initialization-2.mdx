## Uniswap V2 concepts


## Setting the Initial Price

### First Liquidity Provider (LP)

When a pool is created, its balances are zero. The first person to deposit tokens sets the initial price.

### Economic Incentive

The first LP is incentivized to deposit an **equal value** of both tokens. If the ratio they provide deviates from the
global market rate, it creates an immediate arbitrage opportunity, leading to a loss for the LP as external parties
trade the pool back to the market price.

## 3. Mathematical Initialization of LP Tokens

When the first deposit is made, the protocol must determine how many **liquidity tokens** (representing the LP's share)
to mint.

### Geometric Mean Formula

For the initial deposit, the number of minted shares ($s_{minted}$) is calculated as the geometric mean of the deposited
amounts ($x$ and $y$):

$$s_{minted} = \sqrt{x_{deposited} \cdot y_{deposited}}$$

### Rationale

This formula ensures that the value of a single liquidity share is independent of the initial ratio provided by the
first LP. For example, depositing 2 ABC and 800 XYZ (1:400 ratio) yields 40 shares, which remain mathematically
consistent with the underlying value even if the ratio was arbitrary.

## 4. Minimum Liquidity Lock (The "Burn")

To prevent a specific type of economic attack, Uniswap v2 introduces a permanent "burn" of initial liquidity:

### The Problem

An attacker could potentially "donate" tokens to the pool to inflate the value of the smallest unit of a liquidity share
($10^{-18}$ shares), making it too expensive for small LPs to provide liquidity.

### The Solution

Upon the first deposit, the protocol permanently locks the first **1,000 units** (equivalent to $10^{-15}$ shares) of
liquidity by sending them to the "zero address".

### Resulting Math

The amount of liquidity tokens actually sent to the first provider is:

$$Liquidity = \sqrt{x_{deposited} \cdot y_{deposited}} - 1,000$$

This ensures that the total supply of liquidity tokens for any active pool is always at least 1,000, making the cost of
inflating the share value prohibitively expensive for attackers.
